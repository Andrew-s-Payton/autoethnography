If you asked me my opinions on Taylor Swift around five years ago, I would have probably said something along the lines of "I don't care, go away!" Which for as dramatic as I made it out to be, wouldn't be a far fetch from how I felt, and how many others actively feel about her. This isn't unique to just her and is a common phenomenon across nearly every community. I would say it makes the vast majority of people, but also makes up a silent majority. More often than not the topic of Taylor Swift and the community is plagued by two extremes, parasocial superfans, and people would hate with every fiber of their being.

I went to a private high school and many of my classmates were often extremely well-off. Despite this, I would have put myself as being extremely fortunate to go there but I did in a sense feel like an outsider. An aspect that shocked me was often the casualness when it came to going to concerts, but specifically Taylor Swift concerts. Online I knew just how ridiculous and high demand tickets were, causing them to be extremely pricey. When I look back though at my high school experience, I do not once remember any of my classmates ever talking about price, rather talking about the queues and the wait time like it was just some minor footnote. I do believe this did give me a first hand experience with this that many people see online, but do not see for themselves.

I have an online experience with people outside the community that I believe many people share. It is primarily the hatred and witchhunting that those outside the community often feel towards the community and Taylor Swift herself. While I will admit, some of the TikTok I see can be really funny, it often gets to an excessive amount. I feel like people lack a reason for actually disliking her, not actualling caring about the things that she does, more so disliking her because it is trendy. There are decent arguments to make and I will say Taylor Swift and her fans are very far from perfect but often the people making this rhetoric feel more so like second-hand thinkers who do not actually have an opinion.

Many of the friends that I have are those who are also part of the community. An often criticized part of the community that I feel like I have faced first hand is how delusional the community typically is, especially in regards to Taylor Swift dropping music or an album. I have engaged in such discussions with my friends on topics like this often, especially after long moments between announcements. It is painfully obvious that these speculations we often have are melodramatic and serve more so as doing so just for fun, rather than legitimately believing the outlandish theories we propose. While I cannot expand the scope of my interactions to everyone within the community, I do believe it helps counter rhetoric online as often more so an inability to recognize satire most of the time, rather than what I feel like is constructive criticism.

For certain topics that are controversial, such as artists, politics, or even colleges, the majority of people hold either strongly negative or strongly positive opinions. For instance, more often than not a RateMyProfessor score will often appear as a reverse bellcurve, contrasting what would be expected. This can be attributed to a bias, that those who hold strong opinions are far more likely to voice those opinions, really bad or really good professors are more likely to get rated than just average ones. It often manipulates how other people view certain topics as being black and white, rather than a spectrum of varying opinions. The same principle can be extended to Taylor Swift and the community. Online, stronger opinions are far more likely to gather a reaction, and far more likely to be spread. The views from your phone contrasts heavily with the views from reality and it is important to always remember that.

My personal experience in the community has been contrasted by the narratives that outsiders often attempt to portray the community as. Much of the discussion and rhetoric against Taylor Swift and the community has been senseless and without any real purpose. My experiences have shown that the portrayed views of the community are often overexaggerated or cherry picked to spread hateful rhetoric, often for the purposes of popularity or trends. I do recognize however the same biases I believe they have for my community are also biases I share for outsiders, which is equally unrealistic and cherry picked. Demonization from outsiders and idolization from insiders muddy the waters of what the community is actually like. The only way to fundamentally change how we view artists, communities, and the other, taking a more human approach in rhetoric.
